The Golden Ratio
An intersection Of Mathematics, Art, And Yes, Fitness

The Golden ratio, often called 'Phi' and written as φ, is equal to
(1 + √5) / 2 or about 1.618.

The Math

If the ratio of two numbers is φ, then so is the sum of the numbers divided by the larger number. That is if
a / b = φ, then (a + b) / a = φ
(φ + 1) / φ = φ
Numerically,
(1.618 + 1) / 1.618 = 1.618

The reciprocal of φ is equal to φ - 1. That is
1 / φ = φ - 1, or
1 / φ + 1 = φ
Numerically,
1 / 1.618 = 0.618

These equations are equivalent to the quadratic equation x2 - x = 1.
The solutions to the equation are
(1 + √5) / 2 and (1 - √5) / 2 or
φ and 1 - φ = -1 / φ
Numerically,
1.6182 - 1.618 = 1
(-0.618)2 - (-0.618) = 1

Here is a way to create one geometrically. The 1/2 diagonal is equal to
√5 / 2 times the side of the square. Adding this to 1/2 of the side of the square gives us a perfect Golden rectangle.

Some numeric sequences such as the well known Fibonacci Sequence are created by adding new terms that are the sum of the previous two terms. Theses types of sequences have terms whose ratio converges to φ. For example, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13..., the ratio of the last terms shown is (13 / 8) = 1.625.

Artistic Connection

The dimensions of a painting can be in a Golden ratio. This is subjective of course, but this is often considered to be a particularly aesthetically pleasing form. The placement of 'focal points' ie areas of most interest within the painting often follows the ratio.

A Tenuous Fitness Connection

I am trying these sequences as templates for rep patterns. The decreasing rep goals suggest a reduced rest interval in between sets. For example, I like (10, 6, 4) and (13, 8, 5) as a 3 set rep range for a dumbbell squat using a 60 to 90 second interval.

Useful 3 set patterns based on this concept might be a range such as (5, 3, 2), (6, 4, 2), (8, 5, 3), (10, 6, 4), (13, 8, 5), (16, 10, 6), (21, 13, 8), (26, 16, 10), (34, 21, 13).

Some exercises seem to work better than others, if it doesn't feel right, use a more conventional rep range.

Don't overthink this, it is not based on any data that I know of. It just has a nice subjective feel to it.
